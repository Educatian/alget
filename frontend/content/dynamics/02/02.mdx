## Work Done by a Force

$$
\boxed{W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \int F \cos\theta \, ds}
$$

For constant force:
$$
W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta
$$

| Force Type | Work Formula |
|------------|--------------|
| Constant force | $W = Fd\cos\theta$ |
| Spring | $W = \frac{1}{2}k(x_1^2 - x_2^2)$ |
| Gravity | $W = -mg\Delta h$ |
| Friction | $W = -\mu N \cdot d$ (always negative) |

---

## Kinetic Energy

$$
\boxed{T = \frac{1}{2}mv^2}
$$

---

## Work-Energy Theorem

$$
\boxed{W_{net} = \Delta T = T_2 - T_1 = \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_1^2}
$$

> ðŸ’¡ **Power**: The net work equals the change in kinetic energy!

## Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces

| Type | Examples | Work |
|------|----------|------|
| **Conservative** | Gravity, springs | Path-independent |
| **Non-conservative** | Friction, drag | Path-dependent |

For conservative forces, we define **potential energy**:
$$
W_c = -\Delta V = -(V_2 - V_1)
$$

---

## Energy Conservation

When only conservative forces act:
$$
\boxed{T_1 + V_1 = T_2 + V_2}
$$

With non-conservative work:
$$
T_1 + V_1 + W_{nc} = T_2 + V_2
$$
