## What Is Equilibrium?

### The Physical Concept

Imagine a book resting on a table. It doesn't accelerateâ€”it just sits there. Why? Because all the forces acting on it **cancel out perfectly**. The table pushes up with exactly the same force that gravity pulls down.

This is **equilibrium**: a state where the net effect of all forces is zero.

| State | Condition | Physical Meaning |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| Static Equilibrium | $\sum \vec{F} = 0$ and $v = 0$ | Object at rest, stays at rest |
| Dynamic Equilibrium | $\sum \vec{F} = 0$ and $v = \text{const}$ | Moving at constant velocity |

> ğŸ’¡ **Key Insight**: Equilibrium doesn't mean "no forces"â€”it means all forces **balance** each other exactly.

### The Particle Model

In this chapter, we model objects as **particles**â€”points where all forces act at a single location. This simplification is valid when:

- The object's size is small compared to other dimensions in the problem
- Rotational effects can be ignored
- We only care about translational equilibrium

## The Equilibrium Condition

### Vector Form

For a particle in equilibrium, the **vector sum** of all forces equals zero:

$$
\boxed{\sum \vec{F} = 0}
$$

This single elegant equation is Newton's First Law applied to statics.

### Component Form

In practice, we work with components. For 2D problems:

$$
\sum F_x = 0 \quad \text{(horizontal forces balance)}
$$

$$
\sum F_y = 0 \quad \text{(vertical forces balance)}
$$

> âš ï¸ **Critical**: These are **algebraic** sums. Forces in the negative direction carry a **negative sign**. Sign errors are the #1 source of wrong answers!

## Forces in Equilibrium Problems

Before solving problems, you must identify **all** forces acting on your particle:

| Force Type | Symbol | Direction | When Does It Act? |
|------------|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Weight | $W$ or $mg$ | Always **down** | Always, on any object with mass |
| Tension | $T$ | Along cable, **away from** object | Connected to cables, ropes |
| Normal | $N$ | **Perpendicular** to surface | Object touches a surface |
| Friction | $f$ | **Parallel** to surface | Surfaces with friction |
| Applied | $P$ or $F$ | As specified | External forces |

> ğŸ“Œ **Golden Rule for Tension**: Cables can only **pull**â€”never push. Tension always acts **away** from the object along the cable's direction.

## Free Body Diagrams: The Master Skill

The **Free Body Diagram (FBD)** is the most important skill in statics. A correct FBD leads to correct solutions.

### 6-Step FBD Procedure

1. **Isolate** the body of interest
2. **Establish** a coordinate system (x horizontal, y vertical)
3. **Draw weight** acting downward through the center
4. **Draw tension** forces along each cable, away from object
5. **Draw normal and friction** forces at contact surfaces
6. **Label** all forces with symbols
