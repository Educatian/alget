## The Basic Idea

The **method of joints** treats each joint as a particle in equilibrium:

$$
\sum F_x = 0, \quad \sum F_y = 0
$$

Each joint provides **2 equations**, so we can solve for up to **2 unknowns** per joint.

> ğŸ’¡ **Strategy**: Start at a joint with only 2 unknown member forces (often at a support), then work through the truss joint by joint.

---

## Problem-Solving Procedure

### Step 1: Find Support Reactions
Analyze the entire truss as a rigid body to find reactions at supports.

### Step 2: Select Starting Joint
Choose a joint with **at most 2 unknown forces**.

### Step 3: Draw Joint FBD
Isolate the joint. Draw all forces:
- Known external forces/reactions
- Unknown member forces (assume tension: pointing away from joint)

### Step 4: Apply Equilibrium
$$
\sum F_x = 0, \quad \sum F_y = 0
$$

### Step 5: Interpret Results
- **Positive result**: Force is tension (T) as assumed
- **Negative result**: Force is compression (C) (reverse direction)

### Step 6: Move to Next Joint
Use the forces just found as known values at adjacent joints.

---

## Sign Convention

When drawing the FBD of a joint:

| Assumption | Arrow Direction | Meaning |
|------------|-----------------|---------|
| Tension | **Away from** joint | Member pulling on joint |
| Compression | **Toward** joint | Member pushing on joint |

> ğŸ”‘ **Standard Practice**: Always assume tension initially. If the answer is negative, the member is in compression.

## Tips for Efficiency

1. **Exploit symmetry** when possible
2. **Identify zero-force members** first
3. **Choose smart starting joints** (supports often have few unknowns)
4. **Check your work** at the final joint (should satisfy equilibrium)
