## What Is a Truss?

A **truss** is a structure composed of:
- **Members**: Straight, slender elements (beams, bars)
- **Joints**: Connection points (usually pins)
- **Loads**: Applied only at joints

## Truss Assumptions

For analysis, we make these simplifying assumptions:

| Assumption | Implication |
|------------|-------------|
| Members connected by **frictionless pins** | Only axial forces (no moments) |
| Loads applied **only at joints** | Members are two-force members |
| Member **weight is negligible** | Simplifies calculations |
| All members are **straight** | Forces act along member axes |

> ğŸ’¡ **Key Result**: Under these assumptions, every member is a **two-force member**â€”it experiences only tension (T) or compression (C).

## Simple Truss Construction

A **simple truss** is built by:
1. Starting with a basic triangle (3 members, 3 joints)
2. Adding 2 new members and 1 new joint at a time

This ensures the truss is:
- **Stable** (rigid, won't collapse)
- **Statically determinate** (can be solved with equilibrium alone)

### The m-2j+3 Rule

For a 2D truss to be statically determinate:

$$
\boxed{m = 2j - 3}
$$

where:
- $m$ = number of members
- $j$ = number of joints

| Condition | Classification |
|-----------|----------------|
| $m < 2j - 3$ | Unstable (mechanism) |
| $m = 2j - 3$ | Statically determinate |
| $m > 2j - 3$ | Statically indeterminate |

---

## Example: Checking Determinacy

**Problem:** A truss has 9 members and 6 joints. Is it determinate?

**Solution:**

$$
2j - 3 = 2(6) - 3 = 9
$$

Since $m = 9 = 2j - 3$, the truss is **statically determinate**. âœ…
