# 6.1 Introduction to Trusses

In engineering statics, a **truss** is a structure composed of slender members joined together at their end points. Trusses are commonly used in constructing bridges, roof supports, and aerospace structures due to their high strength-to-weight ratio.

### Key Assumptions
To simplify the mathematical analysis of a truss, we make two primary assumptions:
1. **All loadings are applied at the joints.** The weight of the members is often neglected as it is small compared to the applied forces. If a weight is to be considered, it is generally applied as a force at the structural joints.
2. **The members are joined together by smooth pins.** Because of these assumptions, each truss member acts as a **two-force member**, meaning the force within the member is directed along the axis of the member (either in tension or compression).

### The Method of Joints

The **Method of Joints** allows us to determine the internal forces in the various members of a truss. Since the entire truss is in equilibrium, each individual joint must also be in equilibrium.

#### Procedure for Analysis:
1. **Draw a Free-Body Diagram (FBD)** of the entire truss.
2. **Determine the external support reactions** by applying the equations of global equilibrium ($\Sigma F_x = 0$, $\Sigma F_y = 0$, $\Sigma M = 0$).
3. **Select a joint** with at least one known force and at most two unknown forces.
4. Apply the scalar equations of equilibrium ($\Sigma F_x = 0$ and $\Sigma F_y = 0$) to solve for the unknowns at that joint.
5. Proceed to the next joint and repeat the process.

**Remember:** Assume all unknown member forces are in **tension** (pulling away from the joint). If your calculated answer is negative, it means the member is actually in **compression** (pushing into the joint).
