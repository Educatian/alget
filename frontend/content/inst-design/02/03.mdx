## The Evaluation Phase: Measuring Impact

Evaluation is the final phase of the traditional ADDIE model, but in reality, a good instructional designer evaluates continuously throughout the entire process. Measurement is the only way to determine if the training actually solved the performance gap identified back in the Analysis phase.

### Formative vs. Summative Evaluation

*   **Formative Evaluation (During):** This occurs *while* the training is being developed. It acts as a safety net. You might show a storyboard to a Subject Matter Expert (SME) to catch a factual error before filming, or run an alpha test with 5 users to see if the UI is confusing. The goal is to catch and fix errors early when they are cheap to fix.
*   **Summative Evaluation (After):** This occurs *after* the course has officially launched. It measures the final effectiveness and efficiency of the instruction against the original learning objectives.

### Kirkpatrick's 4 Levels of Evaluation

Developed in the 1950s by Donald Kirkpatrick, this remains the gold standard framework for summative evaluation in corporate and higher-ed environments. As you move from Level 1 up to Level 4, it becomes much more valuable to the organization, but exponentially harder and more expensive to measure.

1.  **Level 1: Reaction (The "Smile Sheet")**
    *   *What it measures:* Did the learners find the training engaging, relevant, and useful?
    *   *How to measure:* Post-course surveys, 5-star ratings.
    *   *Warning:* High ratings don't mean they actually learned anything; it just means they liked the course or the instructor.
2.  **Level 2: Learning (The Test)**
    *   *What it measures:* Did they actually acquire the intended knowledge or skills?
    *   *How to measure:* Pre-tests and post-tests, interactive diagnostic assessments, roleplay demonstrations.
3.  **Level 3: Behavior (The Transfer)**
    *   *What it measures:* Did the learners apply what they learned when they went back to their actual job? Overcoming the "knowing-doing gap."
    *   *How to measure:* Manager observations 30 days later, peer reviews, tracking distinct changes in workflow.
4.  **Level 4: Results (The ROI)**
    *   *What it measures:* Did the behavioral change actually impact the bottom line of the organization?
    *   *How to measure:* Tracking sales metrics, monitoring safety incident reports, measuring helpdesk ticket volume before and after the training.

**Real-World Example:**
Imagine a mandatory "Phishing Email Defense" training.
- *Level 1:* Learners rate it 4/5 stars.
- *Level 2:* Learners score 95% on the quiz differentiating real emails from phishing.
- *Level 3 (Failure):* Three weeks later, IT sends a fake phishing test to the whole company. 40% of the employees still click the link because they are rushing and not paying attention. The training failed to transfer to behavior.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Measurement Reality Check:** Moving past Level 2 is notoriously difficult in traditional classroom settings. In modern EdTech, we increasingly rely on behavioral trace logs (time on task, interaction rates, UI clicks) to try and simulate deeper Level 3 insights.
